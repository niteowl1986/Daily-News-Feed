Image not found.Kerala High Court: Bank Cannot Retain Original Title Deeds After Closure Of Loan Account - Verdictum

Published Date: Thu, 12 Jun 2025

The Kerala High Court ruled that South Indian Bank (SIB) lacked the authority to retain the original title deeds of a borrower after the loan account closure. The court acknowledged the bank's acknowledgment of receiving the title deeds as collateral in 2015 but found that no evidence existed to show their return despite the loan account closure in 2023. The court noted the bank's failure to locate the documents and ordered them to either return the original title deeds or initiate proceedings to enable the petitioners to obtain certified copies. SIB was also ordered to pay Rs. 50,000 in costs for wasting judicial time, with Rs. 25,000 going to the petitioners and the remaining Rs. 25,000 to the Kerala Legal Services Authority. The court declined to issue a direction for the release of the title deeds due to their unavailability and reserved the petitioners' right to seek compensation through other appropriate forums. The ruling emphasizes the importance of banks adhering to RBI guidelines regarding the timely release of original title deeds after loan account closure.

Image not found.The evolving green bond landscape - Davy

Published Date: Thu, 12 Jun 2025

The global green bond market has surged to over US$1 trillion in cumulative issuance, driven by increasing regulatory support and investor demand for sustainable finance. Green bonds, defined as bonds financing environmentally friendly projects, are governed by voluntary guidelines known as the Green Bond Principles (GBP), lacking a globally harmonized framework. The Climate Bonds Initiative (CBI) has developed a certification system to address this gap, but it only covers a small portion of the market. The European Union has taken a leading role in regulating the green bond market, introducing a standard for European Green Bonds (EuGB) with strict reporting requirements. Despite the growth and regulatory efforts, green bonds face risks such as greenwashing and market volatility. The future of the green bond market hinges on continued investor confidence, robust regulation, and a globally accepted framework for green bond issuance.
